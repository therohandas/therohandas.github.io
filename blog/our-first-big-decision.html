Our First Big Decision, Already Made for Us

When I was in Class 10, the time came to decide which stream I would take in Class 11. My dad framed it as “the big decision that will shape your future.” For a moment, it felt exciting — like I was finally in control, free to build my own academic path.

Then I saw the options. Physics, Chemistry, Maths. Or Commerce with Maths. Or Humanities with... well, a very limited set of combinations. The “freedom” felt more like being given three preset meal combos at the canteen and told I could pick whichever “I” wanted.

Schools often teach us that freedom is the ability to make our own choices. But what they don't say is that in many systems, the menu is fixed before we even walk in.

On paper, subject choice looks like a democratic process — we get options, we make a selection, and we live with the outcome. But unlike real freedom, the choices are shaped by what's convenient for the institution: which teachers are available, which timetables fit, which subjects “sell well.”

It's a lot like voting in a system where all the candidates come from the same handful of political parties. You can pick your favourite, but you can't create a candidate who actually matches what you believe in.

The irony is that education is supposed to prepare us for the future, yet our early choices are framed in a way that can limit that future. Want to be a journalist but also learn advanced biology? Not possible. Interested in coding but also deeply into history? Too bad — streams don't mix.

Other countries are experimenting with modular systems where students can mix subjects freely, creating combinations that reflect their genuine interests. Here, the system still sees us as “Science kids,” “Commerce kids,” and “Arts kids” — neat little boxes that we're expected to stay in for the next two years at least.

In the end, I picked a stream and moved on, but the whole process left a strange aftertaste. Choosing my subjects should have been about curiosity and ambition, not navigating a pre-set maze.

We talk about preparing students for the “real world,” but maybe the first step is letting us shape our own learning paths. Until then, our choices will keep feeling less like freedom and more like following instructions printed in bold.
